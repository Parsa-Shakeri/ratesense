How RateSense works

RateSense helps you understand what interest-rate changes mean in real dollars: monthly payment impact, total interest paid, and “payment shock” risk over time.

Quick start

1) Use the Calculator

  1. Pick a loan type (Mortgage / Auto / Student / Credit Card).
  2. Enter the balance, interest rate, and loan length (if applicable).
  3. Choose a “rate change scenario” (like +0.25% or +1.00%).
  4. Click Calculate to see the difference.

2) Compare two loans

Use Compare when you want to decide between two options (e.g., 6.25% vs 7.10%, or 30-year vs 15-year).

What the numbers mean

Monthly payment

The scheduled payment for the loan (principal + interest). This is what changes most when rates change.

Total interest

The total interest paid over the full payoff period (or simulated payoff period if you make extra payments).

Payoff time

How many months it takes to fully pay off the balance under the inputs provided.

Mortgage: total monthly housing cost

If you add property taxes, homeowner’s insurance, and monthly fees, RateSense shows your total monthly housing cost:

total monthly housing cost = monthly loan payment + (annual taxes ÷ 12) + (annual insurance ÷ 12) + monthly fees

Charts

Remaining balance

Shows how quickly the balance falls over time. Helpful for seeing the long-run impact of interest rate changes.

Interest vs principal

Splits each monthly payment into interest and principal. Early payments are mostly interest; later payments shift toward principal.

Stress Test (future rate risk)

The Stress Test page is for “what if rates rise over time?” It simulates changing rates month-by-month and shows how high the payment could go.

Rate shocks

You can model simple increases like “+0.25% every 6 months for 2 years.” This helps you understand payment shock risk.

Go to Stress Test

Adjustable-style resets

You can also model periodic resets (like adjustable-rate behavior) with a cap so the rate can’t rise forever.

Note: Stress Test is a simplified model to explore scenarios. Real-world loans can include different reset rules, margins, caps, escrow, and fees.

Live rates

The Rates page can pull public data (like Treasury yields and mortgage benchmarks) so you can start from something close to “today’s market.” If a data source is unavailable, the page may use a best-effort fallback.
